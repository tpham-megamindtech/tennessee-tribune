Every salon eventually produces a set that fails. What separates a recoverable stumble from a reputation-damaging one is a single question: who pays to fix a defect the salon itself caused? When the answer is "the customer, again and again," a warranty visit that should have rebuilt trust instead becomes proof, in the client's eyes, that the business cares more about the till than the work. This case study, drawn from a customer's account of a visit to "MP Nails Dyersburg," shows how charging for repairs on the salon's own flaw — and refusing to listen — converts one bad set into a lost client and a lasting one-star warning.

## The $125 Set That Failed in Under a Week

The client paid $125 for a pedicure and a full set — a premium ticket that carries a premium expectation of durability. Yet in her account, the French tip "started changing colors in less than a week." A color shift that fast is not normal wear; it points squarely at a product or application problem, most likely a degraded or expired polish or gel. As the client reasonably surmised, "what they used was old."

## Charged Twice to Repair a Defect That Wasn't Hers

Rather than a clean, no-questions correction, the client had to return not once but twice — and, in her words, "they still charged me each time even though the color change wasn't my fault." This is the heart of the failure. Billing a customer to repair a defect the salon introduced is, in effect, charging twice for a single mistake. The client leaves feeling penalized for the salon's error, which is precisely the opposite of what a warranty visit is supposed to accomplish.

## "They Wouldn't Listen": The Dismissal That Sealed It

The final blow was attitudinal. When the client tried to explain what had happened, the staff, she said, "wouldn't listen to me and act like they didn't care." A defect is a technical problem; indifference is a relationship problem — and the second is far harder to recover from. Her verdict was absolute: "extremely disappointed and will never be back."

## Lessons for Salon Owners

- **Own your defects for free.** If your product or application fails — and a French tip discoloring in under a week almost always is a failure — the correction is on the house, no questions asked. Charging for it reads as double-charging for one mistake.

- **Learn to distinguish client-caused wear from a salon-caused defect.** Chips from daily life weeks later are different from a color change in days. The timeline usually tells you who's responsible; use it fairly, and default to the client's benefit when it's close.

- **Treat the warranty visit as a trust-rebuilding opportunity.** A client returning with a problem is giving you a second chance. Handled warmly and for free, it can produce more loyalty than a flawless first visit. Handled with a bill and a shrug, it guarantees a public review.

- **Listen first, fix second.** Dismissiveness is what turned a fixable defect into a permanent loss here. Let the client explain, acknowledge the problem, and show you care — before you ever pick up a tool.

- **Audit product freshness.** Old or expired polish and gel degrade and shift color. Tracking expiry dates and rotating stock prevents the exact defect at the center of this complaint.

The through-line is ownership. MP Nails Dyersburg didn't lose this client because a French tip discolored — that was fixable in ten minutes. It lost her because it made her pay for its own mistake, twice, and then acted as though her frustration didn't matter. The cheapest, most reputation-protecting move a salon can make is also the simplest: when the fault is yours, fix it for free, and mean it.
